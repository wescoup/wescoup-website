Full Description

What this is

Open Equation Lab is a public, test-driven effort to identify the smallest possible set of equations that fully describe our universe.

Modern science relies on many overlapping models—classical mechanics, quantum mechanics, quantum field theory, general relativity, cosmology, and chemical interaction laws—each successful within specific domains. Open Equation Lab does not assume which equations are fundamental. Instead, it collects equations that describe real physical and chemical phenomena, evaluates them against shared stress tests, tracks redundancy and subsumption, and iteratively reduces the set toward a minimal explanatory core.

Why this project exists

Science advances by proposing many partial explanations, testing them against observation, retaining what works, and discarding or absorbing what does not. Open Equation Lab makes that competitive process explicit and public. The project asks: what does each equation explain, where does it fail, and which equations survive the widest range of observations with the fewest assumptions?

The end goal

The end goal is conceptual compression: reduce our description of the universe to the smallest possible set of equations that reproduces observed physical and chemical phenomena within stated tolerances. The current Standard Model of particle physics and the Standard Model of cosmology are examples of intermediate compressions—not assumed endpoints.

Why build a large equation library at all?

Although reduction is the goal, selection requires abundance. The library is the method, not the destination: a testing ground, a record of failures and partial successes, a map of what is explained (and how), and a repository of reusable structures for future unifying models.

Scope

Included

  • Physics (classical, quantum, relativistic, cosmological)
  • Chemistry (as the bridge layer between fundamental laws and structured matter)

Excluded (for now)

  • Climate science
  • Biology

These are excluded not because they are unimportant, but because they depend heavily on coarse-graining choices, historical contingency, and boundary conditions far removed from fundamental description.

How it works

  1. Equation submission: contributors submit Equation Cards describing equations that model physical or chemical phenomena.
  2. Stress testing: equations are evaluated against shared sanity checks and observation-linked tests relevant to their stated domain.
  3. Relationship tracking: we record subsumption, equivalence, incompatibility, and complementarity.
  4. Iterative reduction: over time, equations fully subsumed or consistently failing lose standing, while robust structures persist and recombine.

No equation is accepted or rejected by vote. Observations and consistency constraints do the selecting.

The Equation Card (submission standard)

Every contribution is an Equation Card with minimum clarity standards:

  • Title
  • Equation(s)
  • Variables & units
  • Claimed domain (scale + regime)
  • Phenomena claimed
  • Observational outputs (what is compared to data)
  • Minimal test proposal (at least one discriminating check)

Encouraged: limiting cases, known failures/caveats, and relation to existing models. Novel ideas are allowed. Authority is not required. Clarity is.

Stress tests

Universal sanity checks

  • Dimensional consistency
  • Conservation laws (when relevant)
  • Causality constraints (when relevant)
  • Stability under realistic inputs
  • Sensible limiting behavior

Observation-linked tests

  • Orbital dynamics, precession, and weak-field behavior
  • Redshift and lensing behavior
  • Scattering, spectra, and cross sections
  • Diffusion/transport rates and scaling laws
  • Binding energy trends and chemical constraints

An equation is evaluated only against tests relevant to its claimed domain.
